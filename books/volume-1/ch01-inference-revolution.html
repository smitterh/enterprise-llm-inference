Chapter 1 — The Inference Revolution

Author

Hugo Smitter

1 Chapter 1 — The Inference Revolution

1.1 Why This Matters

Every major shift in computing creates a new engineering discipline.

The widespread adoption of relational databases gave rise to database administrators and database engineers. The Internet transformed software development into web engineering. Cloud computing introduced cloud architects, site reliability engineers, and platform engineering teams responsible for operating infrastructure at unprecedented scale. Kubernetes, in turn, reshaped the industry once again by making cloud-native infrastructure its own area of expertise.

Large Language Models represent the next transformation.

Much of the public conversation surrounding AI focuses on training increasingly capable foundation models. Headlines celebrate larger parameter counts, new benchmark records, and ever-growing training clusters. Yet for almost every enterprise, training is not the problem that determines success.

Most organizations will never train a frontier model. Instead, they will deploy, serve, secure, monitor, and optimize models that already exist. Their challenge is not creating intelligence, but making it available to users quickly, reliably, securely, and at a cost that makes business sense.

That challenge is known as LLM inference.

Inference engineering sits at the intersection of distributed systems, GPU computing, networking, scheduling, storage, cloud-native platforms, and machine learning infrastructure. It borrows ideas from each of these disciplines while introducing constraints that none of them were originally designed to address. Traditional software architectures were built around deterministic applications that execute business logic and retrieve data. Inference platforms must orchestrate probabilistic models that generate responses one token at a time while consuming some of the most expensive hardware in the modern data center.

As organizations increasingly embed language models into customer-facing products, internal tools, and autonomous systems, inference is becoming an engineering discipline in its own right.

This book is about that discipline.

2 1.1 Computing Is Entering the Inference Era

For more than three decades, enterprise software has revolved around transactional systems. Although the technologies evolved—from monoliths to distributed services, from virtual machines to containers, and from private data centers to public cloud platforms—the underlying execution model remained remarkably consistent.

A request arrived.

Business logic executed.

Data was retrieved or updated.

A response was returned.

flowchart LR

A[Client] --> B[Load Balancer]
B --> C[Application]
C --> D[Database]
D --> C
C --> A

Nearly every architectural innovation of the past twenty years sought to make this model faster, more scalable, or more reliable. Better databases reduced query latency. Distributed caches minimized repeated work. Load balancers improved availability, while container orchestration platforms made horizontal scaling routine. Despite these advances, the application itself still performed a relatively small amount of computation before returning a complete response.

Language models fundamentally alter this pattern.

Rather than retrieving existing information from a database, an LLM synthesizes new content through billions—or even trillions—of mathematical operations executed across a neural network. Instead of producing an entire response at once, it generates text incrementally, one token at a time. Requests that once occupied a CPU for only a few milliseconds may now keep specialized accelerator hardware busy for several seconds while the response is being created.

This seemingly simple change has profound architectural consequences.

Questions that once dominated application design begin to fade into the background. Concerns such as web server throughput or database connection pools remain important, but they are no longer sufficient. Architects must now understand how to keep GPUs fully utilized, how requests should be scheduled, how conversational context is preserved, and how latency, throughput, and operating cost influence one another.

The central question is no longer “How do I scale my web service?”

It becomes “How do I operate an inference platform efficiently?”

Answering that question requires a different way of thinking about software systems, and it is the foundation upon which the rest of this book is built.

3 1.2 Training Is Not the Business Problem

Public discussion about artificial intelligence often centers on training. Every few months, a new foundation model captures headlines because it contains more parameters, achieves higher benchmark scores, or required an unprecedented amount of compute to produce. The narrative is compelling: the future belongs to the organizations capable of building the largest models.

For most enterprises, however, that narrative is largely irrelevant.

Training a frontier model demands enormous financial investment, specialized expertise, and access to massive GPU clusters operating continuously for weeks or months. Only a handful of organizations possess the resources required to build models at the leading edge of the field. The overwhelming majority of businesses will never attempt to compete in that arena, nor do they need to.

Instead, they consume models that have already been trained.

Whether those models originate from commercial providers such as OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, or Mistral AI, or from open-source communities developing models such as Llama, Qwen, or DeepSeek, the engineering challenge remains fundamentally the same. The model already exists. The question is how to incorporate it into products and business processes in a way that delivers value to users while remaining economically sustainable.

This distinction is easy to overlook because training produces the headlines, while inference produces the business value.

Consider a few representative enterprise applications.

Application Primary Workload
Customer support Millions of inference requests
Coding assistants Continuous inference
Enterprise search Retrieval plus inference
Document summarization Batch inference
Fraud analysis Real-time inference
AI agents Multi-step inference

None of these systems train a model as part of their normal operation. Every customer conversation, every document analyzed, every code completion, and every autonomous agent decision results in another inference request. A successful application may execute millions—or even billions—of inference operations each month without ever performing another training run.

The economic implications are significant.

Training is typically an occasional capital investment. Once a model has been produced, the cost of training is amortized over its useful lifetime. Inference, by contrast, becomes a continuous operational expense. Every prompt consumes compute resources, occupies accelerator memory, and contributes to the infrastructure bill. As usage grows, inference costs grow alongside it.

This shift changes how organizations compete.

Increasingly, businesses differentiate themselves not by owning a unique foundation model, but by operating inference platforms that are faster, more reliable, and more economical than those of their competitors. Two organizations may deploy exactly the same model yet deliver dramatically different user experiences because one has built a superior inference platform.

For enterprise architects, this observation changes the center of gravity. The critical design decisions are no longer concentrated in the training pipeline. They move into the serving infrastructure—the gateways, schedulers, model servers, caches, routers, and observability systems responsible for executing every inference request efficiently.

Understanding those systems is the central objective of this book.

4 1.3 Every Token Has a Cost

For decades, enterprise architects have learned to think about cost in familiar terms. A new application consumes CPU cycles, memory, storage, and network bandwidth. As demand increases, additional servers or containers are deployed, and infrastructure costs generally scale in predictable ways.

Inference systems introduce a different economic model.

Unlike a traditional application, the cost of serving a request is closely tied to the amount of computation required to generate each response. Every prompt initiates a sequence of mathematical operations across the model, and every token produced extends that computation. The longer the interaction, the longer valuable accelerator resources remain occupied.

A simple request such as:

“Summarize this five-page document.”

may appear trivial from the user’s perspective, but it triggers a surprisingly sophisticated execution pipeline. The prompt must first be tokenized and prepared for the model. Memory is allocated on one or more GPUs, the model computes an initial representation of the entire prompt, and only then does text generation begin. From that point forward, the model repeatedly predicts the next token, updates its internal state, and continues the process until the response is complete.

Unlike a conventional REST request, inference is not a single burst of computation followed by an immediate response. It is a sustained workload whose resource consumption grows as new tokens are generated. A longer conversation requires more computation than a shorter one. A more detailed answer occupies accelerator hardware for a longer period. Thousands of simultaneous conversations compete for the same pool of expensive resources.

This changes the way platform engineers think about efficiency.

In a traditional web application, adding capacity is often measured in terms of requests per second or average response time. Those metrics remain important, but they no longer tell the whole story. An inference platform may process relatively few requests while generating an enormous volume of tokens, or it may serve many short interactions that consume comparatively little compute. Understanding the workload therefore requires measuring not only how many requests are served, but also how much work is performed for each request.

As a result, inference engineers begin asking different questions.

How many tokens per second can this model generate?

How efficiently are the GPUs being utilized?

How much does each generated token cost?

Can the same hardware serve more users without increasing latency?

These questions would sound unusual in the context of a traditional web service, yet they become central to operating inference platforms at scale. Throughout this book, we will see that many of the technologies surrounding modern LLM serving—from intelligent schedulers and continuous batching to KV cache management and request routing—exist for one fundamental reason: to maximize the useful work performed by expensive accelerator hardware.

5 1.4 Inference Is Not Just Another API

At first glance, invoking a large language model appears deceptively simple. An application sends a prompt to a REST or gRPC endpoint, waits for a response, and continues processing. From the perspective of the client, the interaction looks much like any other service call in a distributed system.

That similarity ends at the API boundary.

In a conventional web application, the server typically performs a relatively small amount of computation before retrieving or updating data. The response is assembled, returned to the client, and the resources used to process the request are released almost immediately.

flowchart LR

Client --> LoadBalancer["Load Balancer"]
LoadBalancer --> Service
Service --> Database
Database --> Service
Service --> Client

An inference request follows a very different path.

flowchart LR

Client --> Gateway
Gateway --> Router
Router --> Tokenizer
Tokenizer --> Scheduler
Scheduler --> ModelServer["Model Server"]
ModelServer --> GPU
GPU --> Decoder
Decoder --> Stream
Stream --> Client

Before the model can generate a single token, the prompt must be tokenized, scheduled, and assigned to an appropriate model instance. The model then performs an initial computation across the entire prompt before generation begins. Once the first token has been produced, the request enters a decoding loop in which the model repeatedly predicts the next token, updates its internal state, and streams the result back to the client until generation is complete.

This execution model introduces characteristics that are uncommon in traditional enterprise applications.

First, an inference request remains active for its entire lifetime. Rather than completing after a single computation, it continues consuming resources while each new token is generated.

Second, every active request maintains conversational state. The model must preserve information about everything it has already processed so that each subsequent token remains consistent with the conversation.

Third, accelerator hardware remains occupied until generation finishes. Unlike a database query that releases resources immediately after execution, inference workloads may tie up GPUs for seconds—or even minutes—depending on the prompt and the requested output.

Finally, the cost of two requests can differ dramatically even when they arrive at the same time. A short question requesting a concise answer may complete quickly, while another asking for a detailed analysis of a lengthy document can consume orders of magnitude more computation and memory.

These characteristics fundamentally change how inference platforms are designed. Traditional load balancers assume that requests are relatively uniform and that distributing them evenly produces good utilization. Inference workloads violate those assumptions. The scheduler must consider prompt length, expected generation time, available GPU memory, model placement, and the current state of the serving infrastructure before deciding where a request should execute.

Inference platforms therefore require intelligent scheduling rather than simple request distribution. Understanding that distinction is essential because many of the technologies explored throughout this book—from continuous batching and KV cache management to advanced routing strategies—exist to solve precisely this problem.

6 1.5 Why Traditional Scaling Stops Working

Cloud-native engineers have become remarkably good at scaling stateless applications. When demand increases, Kubernetes schedules additional Pods, a load balancer distributes requests among them, and overall throughput grows. Although there are practical limits to this approach, the underlying model is familiar and has proven effective for a wide range of enterprise workloads.

It is tempting to assume that inference platforms can be scaled in the same way.

In practice, they cannot.

The reason is not that Kubernetes is incapable of running language models. Modern inference platforms rely heavily on Kubernetes for orchestration, scheduling, and lifecycle management. The challenge lies elsewhere. Large language models introduce resource constraints that are fundamentally different from those of traditional application services, and those constraints change the economics of scaling.

The first constraint is memory.

A production model may occupy tens or even hundreds of gigabytes of accelerator memory before serving its first request. Unlike a stateless web service, which can often be started in seconds, a model server must load model weights into GPU memory before it can begin processing inference requests. If insufficient memory is available, the model simply cannot execute, regardless of how much computational capacity remains.

This observation surprises many engineers encountering inference systems for the first time. They naturally focus on GPU utilization, assuming that computational throughput is the primary bottleneck. In reality, memory frequently becomes the limiting resource long before the GPUs themselves reach full utilization.

A second constraint is startup time.

Scaling a conventional microservice is relatively inexpensive because new instances can usually be created quickly. Large language models behave differently. Loading model weights from storage, transferring them into accelerator memory, and preparing the runtime environment may take tens of seconds or even several minutes for the largest models. Consequently, inference platforms cannot always respond to sudden demand simply by creating additional replicas.

The workload itself also behaves differently.

In traditional web applications, requests often consume similar resources. A database lookup or account update generally follows a predictable execution path. Inference requests vary dramatically. A short prompt requesting a brief answer may complete almost immediately, while another asking for a detailed analysis of a lengthy document may occupy the same accelerator for many times longer. Capacity planning therefore becomes probabilistic rather than deterministic.

Modern inference engines address these challenges through sophisticated scheduling techniques.

One of the most important is continuous batching, in which requests arriving at different times are dynamically grouped together for execution on the same accelerator. Unlike the static batches commonly used during model training, continuous batching allows new requests to join an active execution stream whenever resources become available. The result is substantially higher hardware utilization without requiring every request to arrive simultaneously.

Another critical innovation is the KV cache, which stores intermediate attention state generated during inference. Without this cache, the model would need to recompute large portions of the conversation for every new token it generates. By preserving previously computed state, the KV cache dramatically reduces redundant computation and improves generation efficiency. At the same time, however, it consumes significant accelerator memory, making cache management one of the central responsibilities of modern inference engines.

These constraints explain why specialized serving frameworks such as vLLM, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM, and llm-d have emerged. Their primary purpose is not simply to execute language models. They exist to manage memory efficiently, schedule work intelligently, maximize accelerator utilization, and reduce the cost of serving inference at scale.

Understanding these constraints is the first step toward understanding modern inference architecture. The remaining chapters examine the mechanisms behind these systems in greater detail, beginning with the execution of a single inference request and gradually expanding to the distributed platforms that serve millions of users every day.

7 1.6 A New Engineering Discipline

The history of computing is, in many ways, the history of specialization.

As systems became more capable and more complex, new engineering disciplines emerged to manage them. Database systems gave rise to database administrators and database engineers. Large-scale networking produced network engineering. Cloud computing created cloud architects, site reliability engineering, and platform engineering. Each discipline developed because existing practices were no longer sufficient to address a new class of technical problems.

Inference engineering is following the same path.

At first, organizations treated language models as another application component. A model server was deployed alongside existing microservices, connected to an API gateway, and managed using familiar operational practices. That approach worked for prototypes and small-scale deployments, but it quickly became clear that production inference introduced challenges unlike those found elsewhere in enterprise computing.

Running an inference platform requires expertise that traditionally spans several disciplines. Engineers must understand distributed systems to design resilient serving architectures, cloud-native platforms to deploy and operate workloads, and networking to minimize latency across increasingly complex request paths. They must also understand accelerator hardware well enough to recognize memory bottlenecks, scheduling trade-offs, and the performance characteristics of modern inference engines.

Equally important, they must understand enough machine learning to reason about how models behave during inference without necessarily becoming machine learning researchers. Most organizations deploying language models are not building new foundation models. Their challenge is operational excellence: selecting the right models, deploying them reliably, monitoring their behavior, and delivering predictable performance under production workloads.

This combination of responsibilities does not fit neatly into any traditional engineering role.

A cloud architect may understand Kubernetes but have little experience with GPU memory management. A machine learning engineer may understand model architectures but have limited exposure to operating highly available distributed systems. Platform engineers are increasingly expected to bridge both worlds, combining expertise from cloud infrastructure, distributed systems, and machine learning operations into a single operational discipline.

That convergence is what this book refers to as inference engineering.

Throughout the chapters that follow, we will examine the principles, architectures, and technologies that define this discipline. Some of the topics will be familiar to experienced cloud-native engineers. Others will be entirely new. Together they form a practical body of knowledge for designing, operating, and evolving production inference platforms.

Like every engineering discipline before it, inference engineering is still evolving. New frameworks, model architectures, and hardware platforms appear with remarkable speed. While the technologies discussed in this book will undoubtedly continue to change, the architectural principles behind them are far more durable. Understanding those principles will allow you to evaluate new tools and techniques without having to relearn the fundamentals each time the ecosystem evolves.

8 1.7 The Road Ahead

This chapter has argued that inference is becoming a distinct engineering discipline. The chapters that follow explain why.

We begin by examining what actually happens when a prompt enters a language model. Before discussing schedulers, gateways, or distributed serving, it is important to understand the mechanics of inference itself. How does text become tokens? Why is generating the first token often slower than generating the rest? Why do context length and conversation history have such a profound impact on performance? These questions provide the foundation for everything that follows.

From there, we explore the technologies that make modern inference platforms possible. We will examine the architecture of contemporary model servers, the role of continuous batching and KV cache management, and the metrics that matter when evaluating serving performance. Along the way, we will develop an understanding of why accelerator memory frequently becomes the limiting resource and why intelligent scheduling is often more valuable than simply adding more hardware.

As the discussion expands, we move beyond individual model servers to distributed inference platforms. We will examine request routing, inference gateways, Kubernetes deployment patterns, observability, benchmarking, and the architectural decisions required to operate inference at enterprise scale. The focus throughout is not on a particular vendor or framework, but on the underlying principles that remain applicable even as the ecosystem continues to evolve.

The goal is not simply to teach you how today’s inference platforms work.

It is to help you develop the architectural intuition needed to evaluate tomorrow’s.

By the end of this volume, you should be able to reason about an inference platform with the same confidence that experienced cloud architects reason about microservice platforms today. You will understand not only how modern inference systems are constructed, but also why they are designed the way they are and how to evaluate the trade-offs involved in building and operating them.

The inference revolution is already underway.

The rest of this book is about understanding the systems that make it possible.

9 Chapter Summary

Large language models are reshaping enterprise computing, but the transformation is not being driven by model training. For most organizations, competitive advantage comes from deploying and operating inference systems that are fast, reliable, secure, and economically efficient.

Unlike traditional applications, inference workloads are long-lived, computationally intensive, and constrained by accelerator memory as much as by compute. Every generated token consumes resources, making scheduling, batching, caching, and request routing fundamental architectural concerns rather than implementation details.

These differences have given rise to inference engineering, an emerging discipline that combines cloud-native architecture, distributed systems, accelerator computing, and machine learning infrastructure. Understanding these principles provides the foundation for designing production inference platforms that can scale to enterprise workloads while balancing latency, throughput, utilization, and cost.

The chapters that follow build on these foundations, moving from the execution of a single inference request to the design and operation of large-scale inference platforms.

10 Key Takeaways

  • Most enterprises create business value through inference rather than model training.
  • Inference workloads differ fundamentally from traditional request/response applications.
  • Every generated token consumes computational resources and contributes to operating cost.
  • Accelerator memory is frequently a more important constraint than raw computational capacity.
  • Efficient scheduling and batching are essential for maximizing hardware utilization.
  • Modern inference platforms require specialized serving frameworks designed specifically for large language models.
  • Inference engineering is emerging as a distinct engineering discipline that combines expertise from distributed systems, cloud-native platforms, and machine learning infrastructure.

11 Glossary

Term Definition
Inference Executing a trained model to generate predictions or content from an input prompt.
Token The smallest unit of text processed by a language model during inference.
Accelerator Specialized hardware, such as a GPU or AI accelerator, optimized for parallel computation.
GPU Graphics Processing Unit, the most common accelerator used for LLM inference.
Continuous Batching Dynamically combining inference requests to improve accelerator utilization.
KV Cache Cached attention keys and values reused during token generation to avoid repeated computation.
Latency The elapsed time required to produce a response or individual token.
Throughput The amount of inference work completed over time, commonly measured in requests or tokens per second.

13 Hands-on Lab 1 — Observing an Inference Request

13.1 Objective

Observe the lifecycle of a simple inference request and identify the factors that influence latency and throughput.

13.2 Tasks

  1. Install a local inference runtime such as Ollama.
  2. Download a lightweight open model.
  3. Execute several prompts of different lengths.
  4. Observe streamed token generation.
  5. Measure:
    • Time to first token (TTFT)
    • Total response time
    • Approximate tokens per second
    • CPU or GPU utilization during execution

13.3 Discussion

  • How does prompt length affect response time?
  • Which portion of the request appears most expensive?
  • Does the model generate all tokens simultaneously or incrementally?
  • Which system resources become heavily utilized during inference?
